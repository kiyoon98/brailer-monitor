Executive Brief

Key Judgments

NDMU's Health Sciences program has a strong interdisciplinary foundation, a useful liberal arts orientation, and an applied community-facing capstone. The main opportunity is to make its Maryland-facing value proposition more explicit through pathways, data literacy, field experience, and alignment with state health priorities.

  1. NDMU is well positioned, but not yet visibly differentiated. The program already combines sciences, communication, ethics, psychology, cultural humility, and healthcare systems content. Peer programs make their distinctive routes more visible through tracks, concentrations, internships, and microcredentials.
  2. The capstone is a real strength. HSC-461 asks students to review literature, interview community members, and develop, implement, and evaluate a health promotion project. That aligns well with current applied-learning trends.
  3. Pathway architecture is the biggest gap. Advisor-guided electives are flexible, but students and external stakeholders need clearer maps into pre-clinical graduate study, community health, behavioral health, health systems, and data-informed care roles.
  4. Data, digital health, and population health analytics need more visibility. National peers increasingly foreground statistics, epidemiology, informatics, AI/data interpretation, dashboards, and health technology.
  5. Maryland gives the program a strong strategic anchor. The state's current SHIP priorities are chronic disease, access to care, women's health, violence, and behavioral health. These can become capstone themes, internship targets, and pathway labels.
  6. The recommended identity is Maryland Health Equity and Community Health Systems. This positioning fits NDMU's Baltimore location, liberal arts mission, ethics orientation, and the region's health system ecosystem.
Design structure informed by public white paper/report conventions from Stanford HAI, MIT Work of the Future, and Harvard Belfer Center.

What NDMU Requires

The major combines anatomy and physiology, introduction to health science, health science communications, legal/current trends, research in health care assessment/planning/evaluation, psychology, and a capstone.

Students also select one science elective and three interdisciplinary electives from areas such as microbiology, teamwork and negotiation, the U.S. health care system, cultural humility, topics in health sciences, and sociology.

What the Program Signals

NDMU's catalog frames the major as interdisciplinary, housed in biology, and aligned with critical thinking, diversity and identity, civic responsibilities, and preparation for employment or graduate study.

Program materials point to roles such as patient care advocate, health services manager, health communicator, health educator, clinical researcher, and public health advocate.

Interpretation: the curriculum has a coherent base, but the public-facing structure still reads as a broad exploratory major rather than a sharply positioned Maryland health workforce program.

Strengths and Gaps

What to Preserve and What to Address

Strengths to Preserve

  • Interdisciplinary structure housed in biology, with liberal arts breadth.
  • Strong fit with ethics, communication, diversity, civic responsibility, and cultural humility.
  • Applied capstone that includes literature review, community interviews, implementation, evaluation, presentations, and written synthesis.
  • Clear preparation for both workforce entry and graduate health professions pathways.
  • Baltimore/Washington location with access to hospitals, public health agencies, community organizations, and health nonprofits.

Gaps to Address

  • No formal track structure visible to students, families, employers, or graduate programs.
  • No clearly required internship, practicum, or fieldwork experience beyond the capstone.
  • Limited visible emphasis on health informatics, AI/data interpretation, epidemiology, population health dashboards, or digital health ethics.
  • Maryland SHIP priorities are not yet mapped directly to course outcomes, capstone themes, or partner experiences.
  • No visible stack of microcredentials or portfolio artifacts that students can show before graduation.
The practical goal is not to replace NDMU's current curriculum. It is to convert its existing strengths into a clearer external promise and add a few high-signal elements that peers already use.
These judgments distinguish catalog-confirmed requirements from broader positioning opportunities.
Maryland Program Positioning

A Stronger Maryland-Facing Identity

Recommended program identity: Maryland Health Equity and Community Health Systems. This gives the program a distinctive regional purpose while preserving pre-health flexibility.

Why This Fit Is Strong

Maryland's 2024-2029 State Health Improvement Plan identifies five priority areas: chronic disease, access to care, women's health, violence, and behavioral health. NDMU can use these priorities as the organizing logic for capstone topics, internship sites, community partnerships, and student portfolio themes.

Baltimore and the Washington region also give students access to a dense ecosystem of hospitals, public health agencies, community organizations, research institutions, and health policy employers. NDMU's existing emphasis on ethics, communication, diversity, and civic responsibility fits that ecosystem better than a generic pre-health major would.

Implementation Roadmap

Action Roadmap

0-6 Months

  • Create a one-page curriculum map linking current courses to Maryland SHIP priorities.
  • Publish three advising pathway sheets using existing requirements and recommended electives.
  • Rename or frame HSC-461 as a Maryland Health Priorities Capstone in advising materials.
  • Add one health data literacy assignment to HSC-373 or HSC-320.

6-18 Months

  • Build an 80-100 hour internship or field experience option with hospitals, local health departments, nonprofits, or community organizations.
  • Add a required or restricted-elective module on health informatics, dashboards, AI/data interpretation, privacy, and digital health ethics.
  • Bundle Mental Health First Aid, CPR/BLS, CHW introduction, or Spanish for Health Professionals into a visible skills portfolio.

18-36 Months

  • Convert advising pathways into formal tracks if enrollment and faculty capacity support it.
  • Create a partner advisory group from Maryland healthcare, public health, behavioral health, and community organizations.
  • Assess student artifacts with a competency map covering evidence, communication, equity, systems, and applied practice.
First priority: build the curriculum map. It will reveal what is already strong, what is missing, and which changes require faculty governance versus advising or co-curricular action.
Roadmap sequencing balances visible program improvement with manageable governance workload.
Caveats and Sources

Caveats

  • This brief is a curriculum benchmarking and positioning analysis, not an accreditation review.
  • NDMU catalog and program pages were prioritized for current requirements; news and ecosystem sources were used for context.
  • The peer trend scan is a limited benchmark sample, so findings should be read as planning signals rather than national generalizations.
  • Credit rules, prerequisites, catalog copy, and Maryland policy priorities should be rechecked before formal proposal drafting.
